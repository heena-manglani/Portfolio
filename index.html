About Me

I am completing the final year of my Ph.D. program in Clinical Psychology from The Ohio State University. I have conducted my graduate research within the Clinical Neuroscience Laboratory under the supervision of Ruchika Shaurya Prakash, Ph.D. Before moving to Columbus, Ohio, I completed my BA in psychology and post-baccalaureate research at Rutgers University Newark with Mauricio Delgado, Ph.D.

The theme of my graduate training has been studying the human body as an integrated system. From conducting whole-brain functional connectivity analyses to designing mind-body lifestyle interventions, I am compelled to study the interactions that underlie health.

Life updates: I am currently completing my Clinical Psychology Residency at Massachusetts General Hospital/Harvard Medical School. As part of the Integrated Brain Health Track, I am delivering clinical interventions to people with neurodegenerative disease (i.e., ALS, MS, acquired neurological illness) and their care partners to build resilience and improve the dyads' quality of life.

Research Interests

My graduate research is centered in neuropsychological assessment, cognitive rehabilitation, and brain-behavior relationships. As the lead project coordinator of a health behavior monitoring (e.g., physical activity and hydration) intervention in MS and facilitator of mindfulness and lifestyle education interventions in older adults, I have gained instrumental experiences in program development and execution.

In pursuit of precision behavioral medicine—identifying which lifestyle interventions are the most effective in improving health for whom—I am intrigued by the mechanisms of health behaviors. Bridging basic and clinical science, I am currently capitalizing on machine learning, multimodal MRI, and various neuropsychological measures to identify robust, clinically meaningful targets for intervention.

Future Directions

I am intrigued by integrative medicine. I am curious about how lifestyle behaviors such as physical activity, mindfulness, and diet impact cognitive and psychosocial functioning. In what ways do integrative practices (i.e., mind-body exercise) enhance the mechanisms of individual behaviors on health? In my future research, I aim to design and test integrative interventions to mitigate symptoms and improve psychosocial function in people with multiple sclerosis.

Publications

Predicting working memory function in MS using an fMRI-based neuromarker

A whole brain functional MRI marker of working memory derived in healthy adults predicted working memory performance in two independent samples of people with multiple sclerosis.

    Manglani, H.R., Fountain-Zaragoza, S., Shankar, A.J.R., Nicholas, J.A., & Prakash R.S. (2021). Employing connectome-based models to predict working memory in multiple sclerosis. Brain Connectivity. 2021.03.01.432930. https://doi.org.

Mindfulness training vs. adaptive cognitive training in Multiple Sclerosis

4-weeks of group-based mindfulness meditation led to faster information processing relative to computerized brain training and a waitlist control in individuals with multiple sclerosis.

    Manglani, H.R., Samimy, S., Schirda, B., Nicholas, J. A., & Prakash, R. S. (2020). Four weeks of mindfulness training vs. adaptive cognitive training in multiple sclerosis: Effects on processing speed and working memory. Neuropsychology, 34(5), 591. https://doi.org.

Brief mindfulness meditation led to lower levels of emotion dysregulation relative to computerized brain training and waitlist control in people with MS.

    Schirda, B., Duraney, E., Lee, H.K., Manglani H.R., Andridge R.R., Plate, A., Nicholas J.A., & Prakash R.S. (2020). Mindfulness Training for Emotion Dysregulation in Multiple Sclerosis: A Pilot Randomized Controlled Trial. Rehabilitation Psychology, 65(3), 206. https://doi.org.

Drug vs non-drug reward-seeking in nicotine addiction

When deprived from both food and nicotine, smokers made more cigarette-seeking than food-seeking responses, suggesting differences in reward-seeking behaviors for drug and non-drug cues during deprivation.

    Manglani, H.R., Lewis, A.H., Wilson, S.J., and Delgado, M.R. (2017). Pavlovian-to-Instrumental Transfer of Nicotine and Food Cues in Deprived Cigarette Smokers. Nicotine and Tobacco Research, 19(6), 670-676. https://doi.org.
